🛡️ WHO & CDC Endorsed Guidelines

COVID-19 Safety Guidelines

Evidence-based precautions to protect yourself and those around you.

Wash hands frequently with soap and water for at least 20 seconds.
Use hand sanitizer with at least 60% alcohol when soap is unavailable.
Avoid touching your face, especially eyes, nose, and mouth.
Clean hands before and after eating, using the restroom, and after coughing.
Wash hands after removing gloves or a mask.
Wear a well-fitting mask in crowded indoor settings or when ventilation is poor.
N95 or KN95 masks offer the best protection against airborne transmission.
Ensure the mask covers your nose and mouth completely.
Do not touch the mask while wearing; clean hands before removing.
Replace disposable masks after each use; wash cloth masks regularly.
Maintain at least 1 metre (3 feet) from others in public.
Avoid large gatherings, especially in enclosed spaces.
Work from home when possible during high-transmission periods.
Prefer outdoor activities over indoor socializing.
Limit contact with high-risk individuals (elderly, immunocompromised).
Get fully vaccinated against COVID-19 as soon as possible.
Stay up to date with recommended booster doses.
Even vaccinated individuals can transmit the virus — continue precautions.
Consult your healthcare provider about which vaccine is right for you.
Vaccination significantly reduces risk of severe illness and hospitalization.
Isolate immediately if you test positive for COVID-19.
Stay in a separate room from household members when possible.
Use a separate bathroom if available.
Wear a mask if you must be around others in your home.
Isolate for at least 5 days; end only when symptoms improve and fever resolves.
Notify close contacts so they can get tested.
Call emergency services immediately for difficulty breathing, persistent chest pain, confusion, or bluish lips.
Contact your doctor if symptoms worsen or do not improve after 5 days.
Seek testing if you've had close contact with a confirmed COVID-19 case.
Use telehealth services when available to avoid potential hospital exposure.
Monitor oxygen levels — below 95% warrants medical attention.

Frequently Asked Questions

The incubation period typically ranges from 1 to 14 days. Most people develop symptoms within 5–6 days. With Omicron variants, it may be shorter (2–4 days).
Common symptoms include fever, dry cough, fatigue, loss of taste or smell, sore throat, headache, congestion, nausea, and diarrhea. Symptoms vary by variant and vaccination status.
You can spread COVID-19 from 1–2 days before symptoms appear until 5–10 days after onset. Peak infectiousness is typically 1–2 days before and 2–3 days after symptoms begin. Asymptomatic cases can also spread the virus.
Yes, reinfection is possible, especially with new variants. Prior infection provides some immunity, but protection can wane. Vaccination alongside natural immunity (hybrid immunity) offers the strongest protection.
COVID-19 vaccines are highly effective at preventing severe disease, hospitalization, and death. Effectiveness against infection may vary with newer variants, but vaccines significantly reduce the risk of serious outcomes.
Long COVID refers to symptoms that persist weeks to months after initial infection — fatigue, brain fog, shortness of breath, and joint pain are common. Studies suggest 10–30% of infected individuals experience some form of long COVID.
